
The history of artificial intelligence stretches back further than most people realize.

In 1950, Alan Turing proposed the famous "Turing Test" as a measure of machine intelligence — if a machine could carry on a conversation indistinguishable from a human's, it could be considered intelligent. This framing shaped decades of AI research.

The term "Artificial Intelligence" itself was coined in 1956 by John McCarthy at the Dartmouth Conference, where researchers gathered with optimistic predictions: that human-level AI was just around the corner. That optimism would be tested by two long "AI winters" — periods of reduced funding and interest after progress stalled.

The modern era of AI began around 2012, when deep neural networks dramatically outperformed classical methods on image recognition benchmarks. Since then, the field has advanced at a remarkable pace, culminating in large language models capable of reasoning, coding, and creative writing.

The central question today is no longer "can machines think?" but "how should thinking machines be built, governed, and aligned with human values?"
